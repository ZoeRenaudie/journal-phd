## *Feux pâles*
### capcMusée d'art contemporain de Bordeaux, December 1990 – March 1991

<div class="two-col">
<div>

**Organized by**
© *readymades appartiennent à tout le monde*®

**Apparent form**
Conventional thematic and chronological survey — 82 artists, 11 rooms, one catalogue

**Actual device**
The agency's director was the artist **Philippe Thomas**. The entire curatorial apparatus was his fiction.

</div>
<div>

**What this makes it**

An *exhibition-artwork*: an institutional presentation that is simultaneously a fictional device — and a test case for documentation.

> "Only on close inspection, and sometimes never, did the mechanisms of its construction become visible."

</div>
</div>

/** Notes **/

Feux pâles was organized by the agency readymades appartiennent à tout le monde at the capcMusée d'art contemporain de Bordeaux from December 1990 to March 1991. The exhibition is an emblematic instance of what I call an exhibition-artwork. Only on close inspection did the mechanisms become visible: the agency's director was in fact the artist Philippe Thomas, and the entire curatorial apparatus was his fiction.

===vvvvvv===

## *Feux pâles* as a network

- **96 artworks** — loaned from 4 continents, from the 15th century to 1990
- **The catalogue** — not documentation of the exhibition, but a constitutive part of it
- **The agency** — a legal fiction with no independent existence
- **The cabinet d'amateur** — a derived work signed by the capc itself
- **Reactivation** — *L'Ombre du jaseur*, MAMCO Geneva, 2014
- **Documentation** — Renaudie 2017, Jaret 2019, Lebovici 2021...

> Exhibitions are not objects. They are **relational and processual events** that survive only through fragmentary, heterogeneous, and situated traces.

/** Notes **/

Exhibitions are not only cultural artifacts but also sites of public, professional, and institutional engagement. Their inherently processual and relational nature resists traditional modes of documentation inherited from object-centered conservation and archival practices.

===vvvvvv===

## Three guiding questions

1. How can exhibitions be conceptualized as **relational and performative** cultural artifacts — and what does this demand of documentation practice?

2. What are the **epistemological limits** of existing heritage ontologies when applied to exhibitions that deliberately destabilize authorship, identity, and temporal boundaries?

3. How can a documentary model accommodate **uncertainty, absence, and multiple equally legitimate situated perspectives** on the same event?

/** Notes **/

These three questions structure the presentation. Using Feux pâles as a sustained case study, I first show what composes an exhibition; I then survey existing ontological frameworks and identify their structural limits; finally I specify what an adequate documentary model in conservation would suggest.

## Exhibitions as *small, complex, and difficult* data

<div class="two-col">
<div>

**What exhibitions are made of**
- Artworks, spatial configurations
- Technical infrastructures
- Institutional constraints
- Professional collaborations
- Discursive framings
- Embodied experiences

They produce meaning **through relationships**, not through stable entities.

</div>
<div>

**What existing systems do**
- Privilege finished objects
- Require named authorship
- Fix events in closed time-spans
- Marginalize collective and informal practices

> "Data are never raw; they are always *capta* — taken, not given."
> — Drucker 2021

</div>
</div>

/** Notes **/

The problem of exhibition preservation emerges from a structural mismatch between conventional heritage data models and the performative nature of exhibitions.

===vvvvvv===

## Existing ontological landscape

<div class="three-col">
<div class="card">

**CIDOC-CRM**
Core heritage model. Strong on provenance and custody. Weak on authorship ambiguity and epistemic status.

</div>
<div class="card">

**LRMoo / FRBRoo**
Work, Expression, Manifestation, Item. Strong on intellectual lineage. Weak on co-constitutive and recursive relationships.

</div>
<div class="card">

**OntoExhibit**
Exhibition discursive dimensions. Strong on curatorial intent. Weak on fictive actors and uncertain provenance.

</div>
</div>

<br/>

> "No model currently proposes an **integrated approach** specifically dedicated to exhibition documentation management."

/** Notes **/

Extensions and specialized ontologies have emerged responding to generic model limitations. We created the Display ontology within l'Ouvroir to focus on topological descriptions of installations.

## The starting point: a documented failure

**2017** — First attempt to document *Feux pâles* using a relational spreadsheet

<div class="two-col">
<div>

**What the spreadsheet could do**
- List 96 artworks with attributes
- Record provenance data per object
- Colour-coded epistemic system
  - Black = verified
  - Blue = uncertain
  - Grey = missing
  - Strikethrough = historically valid, now obsolete

</div>
<div>

**What the spreadsheet could not do**
- Model relations between heterogeneous elements
- Represent competing interpretive accounts
- Express co-constitutive relationships
- Encode epistemic status as queryable data
- Link the documentation act to the object documented

</div>
</div>

> This initial failure is the **starting point** of the inquiry.

/** Notes **/

A first attempt to document Feux pâles using a relational spreadsheet served as a diagnostic instrument: the inability to model relations between heterogeneous elements made the inadequacy of conventional tools immediately legible.

## What LRMoo cannot express

Each alternative solves one rupture and opens another. The choice of mapping is always an **interpretive act**.

| Rupture | What FRBRoo presupposes | What *Feux pâles* does |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Derivation before co-constitution | Catalogue is part of the work, not derived from it |
| **2** | Documentation stands outside the work | Conservation study participates in the work's existence |
| **3** | Identifiable actor for F28 | Authorship deliberately indecidable |
| **4** | Closed time-span for F3_Manifestation | Activation still open in 2024 |

/** Notes **/

Following Goodman's notion of worldmaking, exhibition documentation must support the coexistence of multiple epistemic perspectives without collapsing them into a unified interpretation.

## Experiment B
### Three artefacts — CIDOC-CRM vs OntoExhibit

Three objects selected from the 96 in *Feux pâles*, each probing a different type of limit:

| | Artefact | Problem type |
|---|---|---|
| **n°1** | ® (barcode), capc 1990 | Distributed and fictive authorship |
| **n°4** | Cadran solaire, Thomas Tucher, 17th c. | Uncertain provenance, unresolved restitution |
| **n°85** | *La Collection de M. Venzano* | Fictive person, fictive transaction |

/** Notes **/

These three artefacts are drawn from the documentary conservation report (Tome II, 2017), which already featured a colour-coded epistemic system: black = verified, blue = uncertain, grey = missing, strikethrough = historically valid but now obsolete.

===vvvvvv===

### Artefact n°1 — ® (barcode)
*Acrylic on canvas, 97×130 cm — Inv. 1991-20, capc*

| Fact | CIDOC-CRM | OntoExhibit |
|---|---|---|
| **Authorship**: signed capc, conceived by Thomas via the agency | `E65_Creation` + `P14_carried_out_by` → forced choice: capc? Thomas? agency? | `onto:hasAuthor` — same problem, single authorship presupposed |
| **Hybrid status**: artwork AND scenographic element AND exhibition title | `E22_Man-Made_Object` — no class for co-constitutive role | `onto:ExhibitedItem` — captures display role, not co-constitution |
| **Fictive transaction**: the barcode is the "product" delivered by the agency to the capc | ⚠ No class for a fictive transaction — `E8_Acquisition` presupposes real transfer | ⚠ No class for contractual fictions |
| **Legal status of agency**: "owner" in 1990, but no legal existence | `E8_Acquisition` for 1991 — agency cannot be `E40_Legal_Body` | ⚠ Same gap |

/** Notes **/

The barcode is simultaneously an artwork, the exhibition title, and the contractual product of a legally non-existent agency. None of these three statuses can coexist in CIDOC-CRM or OntoExhibit without forcing an arbitrary choice.

===vvvvvv===

### Artefact n°4 — *Klappensonnenuhr* [Portable sundial]
*Thomas Tucher, Nuremberg, 17th c. — Ivory, bronze — KHM Vienna, Inv. 9826*

| Fact | CIDOC-CRM | OntoExhibit |
|---|---|---|
| **Lender in 1990**: Kunsthistorisches Museum Vienna | `E10_Transfer_of_Custody` + `P28_custody_surrendered_by` → KHM ✓ | `onto:LoanAgreement` ✓ |
| **Current owner unknown**: looted from Clarisse Rothschild, restitution unresolved | ⚠ `P52_has_current_owner` is single-valued — competing claims have no representation | ⚠ No class for disputed ownership or pending restitution claims |
| **Epistemic status** (2017): data valid in 1990, obsolete and contested in 2017 | ⚠ No native mechanism for dating epistemic status — `CRMinf I2_Belief` exists but is rarely implemented | ⚠ No degrees of certainty, no temporality of documentary observations |

> The model either gives **a false answer** or **no answer**. It cannot give an **honest uncertain one**.

/** Notes **/

This is the case most directly tied to my epistemological position as a conservation practitioner. The data "owner: KHM Vienna" was true in 1990. It is false, or at least contested, in 2017. No ontology can encode this without extension.

===vvvvvv===

### Artefact n°85 — *La Collection de Monsieur Georges Venzano*
*B&W photograph, 247.5×360 cm (3 panels) — Gal. Claire Burrus — Inv. 1991-21, capc*

| Fact | CIDOC-CRM | OntoExhibit |
|---|---|---|
| **Displayed author**: [capcMusée] — actually Philippe Thomas fictionalizing the institution as author | ⚠ `E21_Person` or `E40_Legal_Body`: neither can express an institution staged as fictive author | ⚠ `onto:hasAuthor` presupposes a real identifiable agent |
| **Georges Venzano**: fictive "collector" whose name appears in the title | ⚠ No class for non-narrative fictive persons produced by an artistic device | ⚠ No distinction between real and fictive persons in exhibition metadata |
| **Sale circuit**: Gal. Claire Burrus → capc 1991 — but the sale is part of the artistic fiction | `E8_Acquisition` models the 1991 transaction — but erases the fictive dimension | ⚠ Cannot encode a commercial transaction that is simultaneously an artistic gesture |

/** Notes **/

Venzano is the most striking case: the fiction does not bear on the artwork, it bears on the metadata themselves. The title, the author, the collector — everything is fictional.

===vvvvvv===

## Three types of rupture

| Type | What happens | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Forced choice** | Model records the fact but imposes an arbitrary decision | `P14` for ® — who is the author? `P52` for the sundial — who is the owner? |
| **Structural silence** | No class exists for the phenomenon | Fictive non-narrative person; fictive transaction; pending restitution; dated epistemic status |
| **Flattening** | Model records the fact by erasing the meaningful dimension | `E8_Acquisition` for the Venzano sale — the artistic friction disappears |

/** Notes **/

These three types correspond directly to the first requirements identified: distributed authorship, epistemic status, coexistence of legitimate interpretations.

===>>>>>>===

## What an adequate model would require

An exhibition documentary model must be capable of:

1. Representing **distributed and shifting authorship**
2. Integrating **heterogeneous documentary traces** without enforcing artificial coherence
3. Preserving **contradictory or parallel interpretations**
4. Encoding **epistemic status and degrees of uncertainty**
5. Modelling **non-linear and recursive temporalities**
6. Documenting **absences, silences, and opacity** as meaningful conditions — not deficiencies
7. Supporting **iterative enrichment** over time rather than closure through final description

/** Notes **/

Rather than proposing a definitive ontology, this study formulates a set of documentary and epistemological requirements intended to guide future semantic modelling work for exhibitions.

===vvvvvv===

## A warning: not everything should be documented

> "Le droit à l'opacité... n'est pas l'enfermement dans une particularité impénétrable. C'est l'accord donné à ces pratiques du monde qui se proposent à la Relation universelle."
> — Glissant, *Poétique de la Relation*, 1990

**Absence and opacity are legitimate epistemic stances**, not failures to be remedied.

Documentation choices embody values. The structures we build shape what becomes knowable — and what is allowed to remain unknown.

/** Notes **/

This framework also raises a warning: not everything should be documented. Absence and opacity are treated as legitimate epistemic stances, not deficiencies to be corrected.

===vvvvvv===

## Conclusion

*Feux pâles* is not an exceptional case. It is a **particularly legible instance of a broader condition**: exhibitions whose meaning depends on the very instability, multiplicity, and opacity that conventional documentation seeks to eliminate.

<div class="two-col">
<div>

**What this research demonstrates**
- Modelling choices embody values
- Documentation practices shape cultural memory
- Small, carefully structured datasets support nuanced engagement when approached interpretively

</div>
<div>

**The broader stakes**
- Engagement is not only a matter of access
- It is a question of **how cultural phenomena are modelled**
- Whose expertise informs that modelling
- What forms of knowledge the resulting structures **enable or foreclose**

</div>
</div>

/** Notes **/

By working from the inside of this difficulty rather than around it, this research generates a precise map of what a more adequate documentary model for exhibitions would suggest.
